## Design Context

### Users
Crypto Signal Lab is a local-first research terminal for crypto traders and strategy-focused analysts who spend long sessions inspecting price structure, replaying historical conditions, and comparing systematic signals. The primary usage context is a dense desktop dashboard viewed in low-light work setups, with bilingual Chinese and English readability preserved throughout.

### Brand Personality
Sharp, electric, analytical.

The interface should feel like a serious market instrument rather than a consumer finance dashboard. It should project confidence, speed, and intent without tipping into toy-like game UI. Motion and glow can be expressive, but they should always reinforce hierarchy, state, and system feedback.

### Aesthetic Direction
Dark-mode cyberpunk terminal with three reference lanes:

- `Neon trading interface`: high-contrast market dashboard styling inspired by the referenced Dribbble crypto dashboard.
- `Control-room immersion`: layered panels, spatial depth, and cinematic hardware cues inspired by the referenced 3D cyber room.
- `Retro desktop rebellion`: OS-window framing, playful signal accents, and unexpected editorial composition inspired by the referenced Cyan Banister site.

Anti-direction assumptions for this first pass:

- Avoid generic Web3 gradients and purple-blue startup visuals.
- Avoid looking like a shooter HUD or game inventory screen.
- Avoid soft fintech cards and polite SaaS glassmorphism.

### Design Principles
- Treat neon as signal, not wallpaper. Accent color should highlight market state, interaction, and focus instead of bathing the whole UI.
- Keep the dashboard legible under load. Dense data surfaces should stay scannable, with strong hierarchy between title, status, metric, and annotation.
- Make every panel feel instrument-grade. Borders, corners, shadows, and separators should suggest equipment, terminals, and mounted displays.
- Preserve bilingual clarity. Chinese and English text should both remain crisp, stable, and comfortably readable across headings, chips, and metadata.
- Use motion with restraint. Pulses, scans, and hover shifts should communicate live state and interface depth, while respecting reduced-motion users.
